Info

Spironolactone is a drug commonly used to suppress testosterone in trans-feminine people, especially in the United States.

Dosing and Monitoring

100mg to 400mg daily doses are typical for trans-feminine people. Spironolactone works both by changing testosterone receptors and also by blocking the production of testosterone, however, its ability to block testosterone in ways significant to transfeminine people is debated (Lachlan Angus, 2020). Blood tests may only show slightly decreased testosterone levels as much of its impact is at the receptor; This makes monitoring through testosterone testing inconsistent. Many trans-feminine people opt to use bicalutamide or cyproterone acetate for testosterone suppression. When adjusting dosages using 50-100mg steps in dose is typical.

Safety and Effects

While anecdotally Spironolactone can cause unwanted side effects its safety profile makes it rather safe to take. Some common safety concerns for the medications are detailed below.

Spironolactone and Dehydration

As a diuretic Spironolactone causes increased urination for most people and care should be taken to increase fluid intake when starting Spironolactone. Spironolactone also can decrease blood pressure and cause hyponatremia (NH Daily Med). Care should be taken to increase hydration while on this medication and check for symptoms of hypotension.

Spironolactone and Anecdotal Reports of Brain Fog

Research on the topic has not been done, however many anecdotal reports in the community have come about of experiencing brain fog while on Spironolactone.

Spironolactone and Hyperkalemia

Many recommend monitoring of potassium levels while on Spironolactone however in practice the risk of hyperkalemia is rather small and likely not worth testing potassium levels. (Millington Kate, 2019) (Fernandez John, 2016).
